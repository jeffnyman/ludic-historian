Ludic Historian

Computing Eras, Crucibles and People's Computing

In this article, I want to talk a little about the idea of specific computing eras but, even more specifically, a particular bridging element that separates two of the primary eras. This element provided a bit of a "crucible era" that I believe dramatically shaped the future development of simulations and games and thus had an outsized impact on ludic and narrative aspects.

The book Computer: A History of the Information Machine presents an interesting way to frame some history. In August of 1890, the popular American magazine Scientific American put on its cover a montage of the equipment constituting the new punched-card tabulating system for processing the United States Census.

In January of 1950, the cover of Time Magazine showed an anthropomorphized image of a computer wearing a Navy captain's hat which was part of a story about a calculator built at Harvard University for the United States Navy.

In January of 1983, Time Magazine chose for its "Man of the Year" the personal computer.

This all leads to an interesting look at the computer throughout a certain period of history. There came a time when large organizations were turning increasingly to technology as the solution to their information- and data-processing needs.

By the end of the nineteenth century, desk calculators were being mass-produced and installed as standard office equipment, first in large corporations and later in progressively smaller offices and retail establishments. Similarly, the punched-card tabulating system developed to enable the United States government to cope with its 1890 census data gained wide commercial use in the first half of the twentieth century.

Also beginning in the nineteenth century and reaching maturity in the 1920s and 1930s was a separate tradition of analog computing. Engineers built simplified physical models of their problems and measured the values they needed to calculate. Analog computers were used extensively and effectively in the design of electric power networks, dams, and aircraft.

All of these technologies were slow in doing their calculations, required human intervention in the course of a computation, and — at least for many of the most advanced calculating systems — were special-purpose rather than general-purpose devices. This means they could one thing and ostensibly do it rather well; but if you wanted to do something else entirely, you needed an entirely different technology.

Largely due to the needs of militaries in World War II, the push was on to remove those limitations and this saw the emergence of the first electronic, stored-program computers. By 1950, a small number of these machines had been built and placed in operation in military facilities, atomic energy laboratories, aerospace manufacturers, and research universities.

Eventually people started businesses based around building computers for the scientific and business markets. This led to a shift of the machines being used not just for number crunching but also for data-processing and accounting. The market these businesses sold to was government agencies, insurance companies, and large manufacturers.

Thus it was that the early development of the computer industry transformed the computer from a scientific instrument for mathematical computation into a machine for business data processing and more general information processing.

This led to companies attempting to innovate around improvement in components for these machines. This led to faster processing speeds, greater information-storage capacities, improved price/performance ratios, better reliability, and less required maintenance.

There was also innovation around the modes of operation for these machines. These innovations involved the creation of high-level programming languages, real-time computing, time-sharing, broad networking, and various types of human-computer user interfaces, from text-based terminals to graphics-based screens.

The mainframe era was from about 1945 to 1980. The personal computer era started briefly in 1975 and then ramped up in 1977. Between the mainframe and personal eras, however, was the transformational 1960s which introduced time-sharing, minicomputers, and microelectronics.
