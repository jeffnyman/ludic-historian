Ludic Historian

Digging Into History

Scholars and historians have been drilling test wells in what they thought was the bedrock of gaming history for quite some time now.

When you consider story-telling, you become aware that whether we're writing them, telling them, living them, or using them as a paradigmatic way to see and understand the world, a story always has a moment when the plot thickens. In other words, something significant happens. Looking at those moments is crucial.

In his book Serendipities: Language and Lunacy, Umberto Eco recounts how the thirteenth-century Italian explorer Marco Polo searched for unicorns and doggedly claimed to have found them. At the time of Marco Polo, the existence of unicorns was widely accepted in European culture. The belief in unicorns was so strong that people claimed to have found their horns, which were believed to have healing properties and to be able to purify water.

So what did Marco Polo actually discover? One possibility that historians latch onto is that he was referring to rhinoceroses, which were known to inhabit the regions he visited. The Javan rhinoceros, in particular, has a single horn that could have been mistaken for a unicorn horn.

Now, keep in mind rhinoceroses were known to both European and Asian cultures at the time. The animal had been described by Greek and Roman writers, and they were depicted on ancient coins. In medieval Europe, rhinoceroses were sometimes depicted in artwork, and reports of their existence were commonly circulated by travelers and merchants. It leads to the question: how could Marco Polo possibly have confused this animal for a unicorn?

During Polo's time, there were significant language and cultural differences between Asia and Europe, which may have contributed to his misunderstanding of what he was observing. This assumes he observed it at all. Polo may have heard stories or legends about unicorns from locals and interpreted them as real observations.

Accrording to Umberto Eco, however, Marco Polo fell victim to what Eco refers to as "background books." These invisible books, Eco writes, are:

Preconceived notions of the world, derived from our cultural tradition. In a very curious sense we travel knowing in advance what we are on the verge of discovering, because past reading has told us what we are supposed to discover.

Thus, in the case of Marco Polo, background books may have included the widely held belief in unicorns during his time and the stories and legends he may have heard from locals in the areas he was visiting. By relying on these background books, Polo's understanding of reality was shaped by culture, language, and history, which may have led to his mistaken beliefs about unicorns, whether he was told about some "single horned animal" or actually observed one himself.

There is a saying that goes: "We see things not as they are, but as we are." This quote is attributed to Anais Nin, a French writer and essayist. It turns out, seeing is not believing so much as believing is seeing. This can lead to a whole lot of traps in thinking.

What helps us avoid the many traps this leads to is to welcome the state of unknowing. This is where you learn to enjoy the questions more than you do the answers. This also allows you to recognize when certain things may simply be unknowable — and being okay with that, rather than trying to retro-plaster over the cracks in history with a false narrative or a contrived story. That story or narrative may be plausible — it may even be exactly what happened — but we don't know if it's actually true.

One way to get to that state of unknowning is to avoid letting a story or a narrative put you into a state of knowing, albeit quite possible a state of false-knowing. In How History Gets Things Wrong, author Alex Rosenberg says:

The same science that reveals why we view the world through the lens of narrative also shows that the lens not only distorts what we see but is the source of illusions we can neither shake nor even correct for most of the time.

Rosenberg is specifically warning against narrative history here, of which he says:

Narrative history is almost always wrong in a way that science has managed to escape. It's wrong even when a narrative gets the facts of what happened exactly right, without adding things that didn't happen, or leaving out crucial things that did.

It's crucial that we avoid looking back and molding events to fit the beliefs of an already established paradigm, which a narrative often encourages. As such, each element in the history we study is a tile, a little tessera dropped into place — one by one — until a design of history comes into view.

Thus do we get a tessellation that, instead of being a one-sided, overly simplistic view of history, is a view that shows complexity of a particular time period, culture, or event as it unfolded.

The design reflected in this tessellation may reinforce what we've already believed but it equally may challenge what we thought we knew. What we end up with resembles not a predestined master story but more a set of mosaic tiles in the process of being pieced together. As it will usually turn out, many pieces are still missing, and at times, some pieces pop into unexpected places. More accurately, these vibrant, many-shaped tiles work to create not one story, but many.

The picture we get ends up more like the view inside a kaleidoscope, the tiles shifting and the picture changing with each turn of the cylinder. Our evidence is always in fragments, and we can often work to make these fragments only meaningful, rather than whole. Thus we may have locally coherent stories but not one, overarching grand narrative.

Consider the following attributed to Sophocles (in Ajax, lines 1418-1420): 

          Many are the things that mortals,
          seeing, can understand.
          Without seeing, no one can tell
          what the future is likely
          to bring to pass.

This expresses the idea that there are certain things in life that we can only truly understand or comprehend through direct experience or observation. The lines suggest that knowledge and understanding often come from firsthand encounters with the world rather than relying solely on speculation or predictions.

This sentiment resonates with a broader theme found in Sophocles' works and ancient Greek philosophy in general. The Greeks emphasized the importance of empirical knowledge and the idea that direct experience and observation were crucial for gaining wisdom and understanding. This perspective contrasts with the notion that we can fully grasp the future or make accurate predictions without direct engagement with the world.

Sophocles' plays often explored the limitations of human knowledge, the role of fate, and the consequences of actions. This quote could be seen as reflecting the playwright's recognition of the limitations of foresight and the unpredictable nature of the future which is a pretty crucial thing for anyone studying history to come to terms with.

The future is not inevitable; it's often random. At the very least, it's often a matter of a series of accidents, some of which are quite fortuitous in hindsight. And that's important. History allows us to look back from the perspective of the present and find certain meanings regarding past events. But that "meaning" is often patched together from a series of other events that did happen but certainly did not have to happen. Reading history backward can impute a certain "teleology," if you will, into the whole affair.

My project here is an experiment in historical imagination into the possibilities of gaming history. This experiment relies on reading history forward. Too often, we turn to look behind us. We fall victim to the fallacy of the inevitable. Because it happened, it had to happen; we use future events to understand the past. This fallacy is a constant temptation because it moves us back into what we already know, or into what we think we know. Questions like "How did we get to where we are now?" are interested more in the present than in the past.

Reading history backwards blinds us to the open character of the future. When we approach history backwards, we are always informed by the present. We look to the past, seeking our links — here are the steps that got us here; here are the causes that led to these effects, with an emphasis much more on those effects (present) than causes (past).

Reasonable, solid construction starts with foundations. Our experiment is one of foundations, and only of foundations. So if we don't assume we know the future, what can we reconstruct about the lived experiences of diverse peoples across the relevant spatial and temporal contexts that we have to consider?

What happens when we approach our crux points — like the crucible era — forwards rather than backwards? What happens if we start at the very beginning and see what happens from there, taking one careful step forward at a time, with minds open to an unknown, unassumed future?
